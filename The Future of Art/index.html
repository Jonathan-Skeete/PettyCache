The Origin of Art

Thousands of years ago, a man drew his own shadow; he took up a burnt stick and drew around it. He started to draw stick figures in the caves. This is how art got started. This legend refers to caveman drawings that are more than 30,000 years old. People then did not see it as art, but today we see drawing as a form of art. Most historians agree that if art had one place for where it started, it would be in Africa; African people who lived more than 70,000 years ago have drawn art and art objects.Art is one of those things that does not have a specific date or time when it started. Since the beginning of time, human beings have had this natural creative ability. Whether it was the caveman carving into the stones of a cave or someone wanting to sculpt or mold a lump of rock into something beautiful, we have wanted to find a way to create art since the beginning of time.

Leonardo Davinci (15 April 1452 - 2 May 1519)

Leonardo is identified as one of the greatest painters in the history of art and is often credited as the founder of the High Renaissance. Despite having many lost works and fewer than 25 attributed major works—including numerous unfinished works—he created some of the most influential paintings in Western art. His magnum opus, the Mona Lisa, is his best known work and often regarded as the world's most famous painting. The Last Supper is the most reproduced religious painting of all time and his Vitruvian Man drawing is also regarded as a cultural icon. In 2017, Salvator Mundi, attributed in whole or part to Leonardo was sold at auction for US $450.3 million, setting a new record for the most expensive painting ever sold at public auction.

During the time of the Renaissance (1495 - 1597)

Eugene Delacroix (26 April 1798 - 13 August 1863)

As a painter and muralist, Delacroix's use of expressive brushstrokes and his study of the optical effects of color profoundly shaped the work of the Impressionists, while his passion for the exotic inspired the artists of the Symbolist movement. A fine lithographer, Delacroix illustrated various works of William Shakespeare, the Scottish author Walter Scott, and the German author Johann Wolfgang von Goethe.

During the time of the Romanticism (1800 - 1850)

Vincent Van Gogh (30 March 1853 - 29 July 1980)

Van Gogh's early works consisted mostly of still lifes and depictions of peasant labourers. In 1886, he moved to Paris, where he met members of the artistic avant-garde, including Émile Bernard and Paul Gauguin, who were seeking new paths beyond Impressionism. Frustrated in Paris and inspired by a growing spirit of artistic change and collaboration, Van Gogh moved to Arles in south of France in February 1888 with the goal of establishing an artistic retreat and commune. Once there, Van Gogh's art changed. His paintings grew brighter and he turned his attention to the natural world, depicting local olive groves, wheat fields and sunflowers. Van Gogh invited Gauguin to join him in Arles and eagerly anticipated Gauguin's arrival in the fall of 1888.

During the time of Modern Art (1860s - 1870s)

Pablo Picasso (5 October 1881 - April 8 1973)

Picasso's work is often categorized into periods. While the names of many of his later periods are debated, the most commonly accepted periods in his work are the Blue Period (1901-1904), the Rose Period (1904-1906), the African-influenced Period (1907-1909), Analytic Cubism (1909-1912), and Synthetic Cubism (1912-1919), also referred to as the Crystal period. Much of Picasso's work of the late 1910s and early 1920s is in a neoclassical style, and his work in the mid-1920s often has characteristics of Surrealism. His later work often combines elements of his earlier styles.

Carlo Carro (February 11, 1881 - April 13, 1966)

He is best known for his 1911 Futurist work, The Funeral of the Anarchist Galli. Carrà was indeed an anarchist as a young man but, along with many other Futurists, later held more reactionary political views, becoming ultranationalist and irredentist before and during the war. He supported fascism after 1918. In the 1930s, Carrà signed a manifesto which called for support of the state ideology through art. The Strapaese group he joined, founded by Giorgio Morandi, was strongly influenced by fascism and responded to the neo-classical guidelines which had been set by the regime after 1937, but was opposed to the ideological drive towards strong centralism. Carrà died in Milan in 1966.

During the time of Contemporary art 1978 - Present

Jean-Michel Basquiat (December 22, 1960 - August 12, 1988)

Basquiat's art focused on dichotomies such as wealth versus poverty, integration versus segregation, and inner versus outer experience. He appropriated poetry, drawing, and painting, and married text and image, abstraction, figuration, and historical information mixed with contemporary critique. He used social commentary in his paintings as a tool for introspection and for identifying with his experiences in the black community, as well as attacks on power structures and systems of racism. His visual poetics were acutely political and direct in their criticism of colonialism and support for class struggle.

The Future of Art

"The art of the future will be transformed by holographic technology. Museums and galleries will display stunning 3D holographic works that seem to float in mid-air without any screens or supports. Holographic art will range from intricate sculptures that visitors can walk around and view from all angles to massive immersive installations that envelop the viewer in surreal dreamscapes of light and motion. Artists will create holographic living paintings that subtly shift and evolve over time. The imagery will change based on the viewer's perspective, adapting itself in real time to look different from various angles. Abstract geometrical shapes will kaleidoscopically transform as people interact with the artwork. Outdoor holographic art will also become popular. Cities could have entire holographic districts where fantastical alien landscapes, psychedelic visions, and surreal cityscapes are projected onto buildings and spill out onto the streets. Holographic creatures and characters will roam these urban wonderlands, playing and interacting with delighted tourist. Performance art will be reinvented by holograms. Holographic actors will star in avant-garde holographic theatre productions. Musicians could perform as volumetric holograms on stage, transforming in appearance during a concert. Holographic ballerinas and acrobats will amaze audiences with supernatural feats. The art of the future will break free of physical constraints and open portals to new dimensions using holograms. Artists will guide viewers into impossible worlds where the imagination can roam freely. Holographic art will profoundly change how people connect with art and alter the very notion of reality." - Sulaiman Dem-Zerigat

"Technology has evolved into one of the main powerhouses of the world, so too has Art. Art has transitioned throughout time beginning with cavemen drawing their shadows to the vibrant and expressive forms we see today. Generally, there is no distinctive description of what art will look like in the future due to the constant evolution and fast paced changes of our world, but I do think these evolutions in technology will play a big part in how art will be constructed and presented. I believe art will develop into a digital realm that will provide unique experiences not only for the developer but also its viewers. It will be focused on a more visual and intangible form more so than traditional paintings or drawings. AI will play a big role in how it is presented and provide experiences for viewers that would be easily accessible along with a vibrant and clear cut imaging." - Kalisa Mckenzie

"Art has always been a key contributor to the advancement of civilization since the beginning of time. Whether it's paintings, sculptures, cinema or music, artist expression promotes community. And just like any community or civilization art has and will continue to evolve or time. Technology shares the same significance and evolutionary expectation as art. Transitioning from a chisel and stone to a digital tablet and pen is synonymous with going from a rotary phone to a smart phone. In today's world we have artificial intelligence, 3D printers, and various software that can produce pieces of art that many find entertaining. The future of art and technology are unbound and the collaboration of the two make for an exciting experience. However, conversations can be had about the authenticity of modern art especially when its production is faster and more readily available by AI. In my opinion art in not art without the passion of its creator. For art to remain pure and authentic, the artists of the world need to remain passionate about their work and use technology to aid in their artistic expression. Not replace it." - Jonathan Skeete
